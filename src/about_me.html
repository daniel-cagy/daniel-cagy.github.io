About Me

Biography

Hello! I'm Daniel Cagy — an Artificial Intelligence student at PUC-Rio, Brazil, focused on software engineering, cybersecurity, embedded systems, and web development. I enjoy integrating software and hardware to solve real-world problems and create better user experiences.

I work mainly with Python, Rust, C, C++, and, of course, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — but I’m always exploring new technologies and programming paradigms.

Hobbies

I’d like to share a few of my main hobbies, since they play a big role in shaping how I think and create.

Coding

The obvious one first.

I started programming at 16 after watching the CS50 lectures on YouTube — and I’ve been hooked ever since. Solving problems, building from scratch, and watching an idea come alive after hours of debugging is deeply satisfying to me.

Gaming

I enjoy mostly single-player, story-driven, and strategy/simulation games. They’re not just entertainment — they inspire creativity and strategic thinking, both of which often influence how I approach my projects. One of my earliest motivations for learning to code was actually to create my own games someday.

Music

I’m passionate about progressive rock and metal, though my taste is fairly broad. Music helps me relax and focus, especially while coding — and sometimes, complex music even inspires complex code.

Training

I also love weight training. It balances my sedentary coding routine and clears my mind — it’s my reset button before diving back into technical work.

Programming Philosophy

I believe programming isn’t just about writing code — it’s about writing clean, efficient, and maintainable code. I constantly try to refine my skills and follow best practices to become a better engineer.

I’m also a big fan of terminal-based environments like Vim and Neovim — they keep me fast and focused. Even when I use full IDEs like VSCode, CLion, or PyCharm, I always enable Vim motions — it just feels right.

I think software should be optimized, even in small projects. Many people assume optimization only matters for large-scale systems, but I disagree — bloated software ruins user experience at any scale. My goal is to write code that’s efficient and readable, finding the sweet spot between performance and clarity.

Overall, programming is more than a skill to me — it’s how I think, learn, and express ideas.
